The Evolution of the World Wide Web

THE HISTORY OF

THE WEB

1946

A logic Named Joe

The American writer Murray Leinster publishes a science fiction novel "A logic Named Joe". In this work he predicts a future where every home on the planet will have its own 'Logic', a kind of connected terminal allowing to play, to learn, to work, to communicate. While that same year, the first fully electronic computer to be presented to the general public weighed 30 tons and had a wingspan of 72m2 and was designed to calculate artillery firing tables.

1955

Birth of Tim Berners-Lee

1969

The beginnings of the internet

Started in 1969 by the research agency of the American army (DARPA) Internet is the technical network that allows the exchange of communication, It is a project, in 1969, which seeks to create a first link between 4 computers located in American universities and research centers to communicate between scientists and share documentation. This network will become more complex and will become the network of networks thanks to important steps such as the implementation of the TCP/IP protocol (this will explain why the Internet is becoming a network of networks that aggregates other networks that will soon become global and allow communication).

1980

"ENQUIRE" project

Tim Berners-Lee, who became a computer engineer at CERN (European Center for Nuclear Research), joined the "ENQUIRE" project. The first goal was to create a database for nuclear researchers. This project was not intended for the general public

1989

World Wide Web

following his work in the "ENQUIRE" project, Tim Berners-Lee wrote the first proposal for the creation of the World Wide Web intended to facilitate access to information for researchers through hypertext links and interconnected computers and thus to allow the instantaneous sharing of information between physicists working around the world.

How to manage a growing documentation and how to exchange information in a localized, reliable and precise way ?
The web is a way to alleviate the fear of drowning in information.

1990

Robert Cailliau

He is joined by the Belgian systems engineer Robert Cailliau who at that time was working on a hypertext system project. Together they decided on the fundamental concepts and defined the main terms related to the Web.

The web uses the internet to do 3 things:

Locate data

URL PROTOCOL

Transmit data to the graphical interface

HTTP PROTOCOL

Describe the data to the browser

HTML PROTOCOL

1992

IN2P3

The first French website is born, it is the 5th worldwide. This website was created to meet the needs of the physics laboratory IN2P3 (Institut national de physique nucléaire et de physique des particules) in Lyon.

Two CNRS engineers, Wojciech Wojcik and Daniel Charnay, working at the IN2P3 computing center, set up the first server.

The computing center has always worked hand in hand with CERN. In 1992, during a conference on high energy organized by CERN and the CNRS in Annecy, Wojciech Wojcik, who was co-organizer of this conference, was able to attend the presentation of Tim Berners-Lee who presented his invention for the first time. Following this conference, Wojciech Wojcik, returns to the CC-IN2P3 with the technology in his pocket and installs the server, based on the model of the one at CERN, on a NeXTcube station, the same computer used at CERN. The first French Web server was born.

Between late 1992 and early 1993

Mosaic

The first browser called Mosaic is born and the source code of the web is published. Suddenly, anyone can create a website, anyone can provide a computer to become a web server, and connect to that content, create it themselves and link to it.

1994

W3C

To make his creation evolve Tim Berners-Lee joined MIT and founded the World Wide Web Consertium (W3C), to deliver the standards of a free and transparent open web. Any legal entity can become a member and come to discuss technical standards and what they should be.

It is a place of debate, in the form of a mailing list. Any legal entity can become a member of the W3C by paying a price that can go up to 60k$ today, for the biggest companies, but which is much lower for associations. And it's from there that you become an active member of certain working groups, on certain protocols. We participate in mailing lists by saying what it should be, a kind of document that is constantly discussed. There are also regular (Plenary Assemblies) to meet physically and continue the discussion in front of each other.

1995

the WWW becomes the main attraction.

This is where the web 1.0 appeared, it is the initial web, which is today called the "static" web. This first generation web is made of static pages presenting a limited content of hypertext or hypermedia, rarely updated and in "read only" mode The web is treated as a means to disseminate documents, to distribute information. These are very simple pages, not necessarily complex to produce. Today, having your own publication medium, having a real independent web site, has become much more complicated. There are a lot of standards and constraints to respect, and the referencing of these sites is also harmed if the site does not belong to a large group. It is this same year that Javascript and CSS1 were introduced. Javascript opened the way to a more dynamic and interactive web.

1998

WIFI

WIFI was introduced to the general public by the IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers)

1998

Google

The start-up Google is born, it was founded by two students in computer science of the American university Stanford, Larry Page 24 years and Sergey Brin 23 years.

Open Diary

Open Diary allows users to publish their diaries online publicly or privately and add comments

End of 1998

Google

Google's search engine processes 10,000 queries daily.

1999

Google

25 million dollars are invested by Sequoia Capital and Kleiner Perkins Caufields & Buyers. At the end of the summer, Google records three million searches daily. On September 21, the test phase of the engine is over. Word-of-mouth takes care of the success of the engine.

early 2000s

new era for the web.

It is then that we notice the arrival of "web empires" that understand that there are resources to capture online, like the attention of users.

2000

Wikipedia

Wikipedia was launched, 20,000 articles are published in the first year.

2003

Linkedin

Linkedin was launched

Web 2.0

more participative web

The creation of blogging platforms, the appearance of Wikipedia, will bring the web into a new era, the web 2.0. The initial idea of web 2.0 is a more participative web, it is for Internet users to produce and share content. It relies on collective intelligence. Blogs, podcasts, file exchange, customizable sites, social networks, are a democratization of creativity, editing, and writing. The Internet user actively participates instead of passively consuming. They become spectators, actors and commentators at the same time.

2004

XHTML

Tim Berners-Lee wants to develop XHTML, to make the semantic web, that is to say to give meaning to data, if there is a number on the web, to say if this number corresponds to something precise and to surround, thanks to a protocol, the information, to give it meaning.

2004

Facebook was launched

The social network created by Mark Zuckerberg has become a monster gathering billions of users, far from the student's paper clip that it was at the beginning. So much so that it has now become the target of all the questions concerning the preservation of everyone's personal data. Moreover, Tim Berners Lee does not appreciate the service, which he considers to be a "Web within the Web" that could end up fragmenting his invention.

2005

Youtube

Youtube, the video sharing site, is revolutionizing video consumption on the Web. Google buys the promising platform a year and a half after its launch, for 1.65 billion dollars...

What future for the web?

Also known as the "decentralized Web," Web 3.0 represents the latest generation of Internet applications and services powered by distributed ledger technology, the most common of which is the blockchain. However, this is not a new concept. The inventor of the World Wide Web himself, Tim Berners-Lee, predicted that this version of the internet, which he called the Semantic Web, would be more open, intelligent and autonomous.

The problem of the Semantic Web is to give information to the computer so that it is not looking for a correspondence between two words, because it risks coming across too many coincidences to be able to get something useful and interesting out of it, but rather to look for correspondences between concepts that have been linked together and enriched so that when the computer goes to look for correspondences, it can find intelligent ones.

That the computer is able to interconnect the information to find not only identical concepts, but also neighboring concepts.

Web 3.0 is based on new technologies such as the Blockchain and the metaverse, with decentralization as its main advantage.

Decentralization is a characteristic of Web 3.0, which implies that the network is powered by millions of computers located all over the world, rather than by a few data centers owned by companies. This decentralized network is inspired by blockchain technology - the underlying technology that powers Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies.

Blockchain is a kind of digital ledger where digital records are kept without the intervention of a central authority. It already allows today to buy and sell goods and services without going through traditional financial institutes like banks.

But how could blockchain be applied to this decentralization of the web? Let's take the example of Uber drivers who work almost independently, thanks to the application that connects them with their customers.

Blockchain could allow these drivers to no longer depend on Uber or any other company, but simply on a computer system that would automatically manage all the processes necessary for their activity. Money would then flow directly from the customer's wallet to the driver's. The advantage of the blockchain is its high degree of security because its register is not held on the servers of a single company but copied identically on all the devices of the participants of this network.

As for the metaverse, it "refers to the future of the Internet, a virtual reality world in which users can interact". This concept is developing very quickly, and we are seeing big investments from the technology giants. Facebook recently changed its name to "Meta".

"Web 3.0 supports decentralization, and the metaverse allows anyone to move freely in the metaverse and exchange assets transparently."

With so many possibilities then, each company in the digital industry is defining its own vision of what role the metaverse should play in Web 3.0.

Microsoft, Facebook and Apple have decided to develop metaverses specific to their respective communities. Rather than being disrupted by other players.

With Web 3 there is a kind of return in terms of value to Web 1, each user had the power to write content and to be heard, there was then with Web 2 a recentralization with actors like GAFAM, Web 3 is a bit the return of the user who regains some power over its content and its data, as Tim Berners-Lee had thought. However, in the face of this, the responses of the digital giants are massive investments in the creation and development of metaverses. The control of users' data via the control of metaverses is a real economic stake for these companies and this suggests that the total decentralization wanted by Web 3 is still far from being effective.
